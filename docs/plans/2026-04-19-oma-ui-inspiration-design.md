## What Your Inspiration Board Actually Says

Your board is much more specific than "make it modern." It points to a narrow design taste:

- You prefer dark, instrument-like mobile shells more than airy SaaS light mode.
- When a screen is light, it is still structured like a premium product, not a generic admin panel.
- You repeatedly save layouts with one dominant hero surface, a small number of supporting cards, and a floating bottom nav.
- You like accent colors used sparingly. Lime, electric blue, soft iridescent gradients, and warm orange show up as focus tools, not as wall-to-wall decoration.
- You respond to bold numerics, large headings, thick card radii, and compact chip controls.
- You save mobile UIs that feel tactile and guided: bottom sheets, contextual menus, quick action clusters, and obvious next steps.
- You do not save many dense spreadsheet-like enterprise screens. Even your data-heavy references turn dashboards into layered mobile instruments.

The important conclusion is this: your target is not "clean corporate mobile." Your target is "premium mobile command center." The UI should feel confident, fast, and sharply prioritized.

## Patterns That Repeat Across the Board

- **Dark first, light second:** most references are dark, and the light references are still controlled and contrast-led.
- **One hero, then support cards:** the best screens show one strong story first and a small number of lower-priority cards after it.
- **Floating navigation:** the board repeatedly favors rounded floating bottom navigation with a strong active state.
- **Action scarcity:** users get a few obvious actions, not a screen full of equal buttons.
- **Big numbers, short labels:** numerics carry the weight. Supporting text stays short.
- **Accent discipline:** lime, blue, orange, and gradient highlights are used to focus attention, not decorate every surface.
- **Operational dashboards without spreadsheet aesthetics:** even dense flows still feel like mobile products, not compressed desktop reports.
- **Contextual behavior:** bottom sheets, contextual menus, and local toolbars appear often.
- **Atmosphere with restraint:** glows and gradients exist, but they sit behind the hierarchy instead of replacing it.

## Expert Guidance That Fits Your Board

The expert material aligns with the board on several non-negotiable points:

- Keep bottom navigation to five primary destinations at most, and make the touch targets large.
- Let top actions change by context instead of pinning every action on every screen.
- On mobile, one section should move in one direction. Do not build mini desktop dashboards with both vertical and horizontal complexity at once.
- Treat cards as the main grouping primitive, but avoid card-inside-card-inside-card stacks when whitespace can separate content.
- Make most screens do one job. The home screen can be broader, but the rest should be narrower.
- Use bottom sheets for secondary selection flows so users stay in context.
- Design gestures and transitions as part of the product, not as polish added later.
- Build strong empty states and zero-search-result states. Do not leave blank panels.

## What Makes The Current UI Feel Outdated Or Scattered

The current app is not ugly. The problem is that it feels halfway done.

- The app already has a redesign language, but it is a **prototype language**, not a finished product language.
- Every major route uses the same soft blue glow, rounded white card, and explanatory intro block. That creates consistency, but also sameness.
- The current screens spend too much space telling the user what the page is, and not enough space showing the most urgent next action.
- Several flows violate the expert rule that one screen should mostly do one job. The intro, summary, filter controls, and content list often compete at the same level.
- The bottom nav currently exposes **six primary destinations** in `components/oma/OmaFloatingNav.tsx`, while the expert guidance says five is the hard limit and three or four is better.
- The board wants decisive accent use. The app currently mixes pale blue circles, black pills, navy cards, green chips, red states, and white cards without a single dominant system story.
- Motion is mostly absent from the current screenshots, but your board repeatedly values contextual action reveals, bottom sheets, and gesture-aware flows.
- The app reads as **screen-local styling** rather than a reusable design system. That creates drift.

## Home Page Direction

The home page should become the first place where the new system is fully visible.

### What the current home does wrong

- It greets the user well, but it behaves like a clean dashboard template.
- It introduces too many equal surfaces too quickly.
- Quick actions and action hub cards feel generic instead of role-specific.
- The hero card gives a revenue number, but it does not create tension around what matters now.

### What the new home should do

- Lead with one high-contrast hero that answers: "What needs attention right now?"
- Show three manager signals max under the hero.
- Show one queue preview section, not multiple competing utility blocks.
- Keep quick actions to the smallest useful set.
- Move secondary discovery into a bottom sheet, overflow action, or contextual search.
- Use a floating nav with five visible primary items max. Keep route access to all existing screens, but do not force all of them into primary navigation at once.

### Recommended home hierarchy

1. Top row: identity, search, notifications, overflow.
2. Hero: booked demand or revenue-at-risk, plus one clear delta and three compact stats.
3. Manager signals: approvals, dispatch pressure, collections risk.
4. Queue preview: the next actionable orders.
5. Recent movement: recent approvals, dispatches, and exceptions.
6. Optional quick actions: two or three, not four to six.

### Recommended visual language

- Use a dark graphite shell for the home screen.
- Use electric blue as the primary home accent.
- Use green, orange, and red only for semantic states.
- Keep one glow behind the hero and nowhere else.
- Make the active nav pill bold and obvious.
- Replace decorative background circles with more intentional surface contrast.

## Design Rules For The Redesign

- Build the system around mobile-first cards, not desktop-style panels.
- Stop explaining each page at length. Use shorter labels and let the data carry the screen.
- Increase type confidence. Use larger headings, stronger numerics, and fewer midweight text blocks.
- Let one accent control each screen.
- Use bottom sheets for search, pickers, and overflow actions where context matters.
- Design empty states, loading states, and error states as part of the system.
- Keep route behavior and backend logic intact while changing the visual shell.
- Start with the home screen as the pilot. If the home screen does not feel materially different, the rest of the migration will drift back to the current safe pattern.
